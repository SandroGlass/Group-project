The number of nuclear weapons in the world increased this year for the first time in decades.

China is the clear leader in nuclear expansion, having tripled its nuclear arsenal to 600 in the last six years.

The UK’s expansion is more subtle. It raised the limit on how many nuclear weapons it can hold from 225 to 260.

Ukraine, a country torn by war, no longer has a nuclear arsenal to increase. Today, many Ukrainians want it back.

Meanwhile, in Japan - the only country ever attacked with nuclear bombs - survivors are urging the world to remember the past and resist repeating it.

These increase and tensions are among the reasons why the scientists moved the Doomsday Clock, which warns about humanity's danger of self-destruction, closer to midnight than ever before.

The global number of nuclear stockpiles is growing for the first time since 1986. China, India, Pakistan, and North Korea are increasing their arsenals.

Meanwhile, the United Kingdom has raised its cap on nuclear weapon holdings from 225 to 260.

Some countries are discreetly increasing their stockpiles

Source: Federation of American Scientists

Britain is now prioritizing expansion "without much transparency" about what weapons remain operational versus those in storage, Dave Cullen of BASIC, a London-based think tank, says.

Rob Green, lead nuclear regulator at the Environment Agency, doesn’t expect the UK numbers to drop any time soon.

“The only time that would ever change is if UK government policy changed, and they said, ‘Well, you know what? We don’t need a nuclear weapon anymore.’” he says.

Although Russia and the US are reducing their vast arsenals, countries are not dismantling as many weapons as they used to.

As some countries increase their stockpiles, or slow down the rate at which they decommission, embattled Ukraine wants to take back the nuclear weapons it gave up as the Cold War was ending.

Ukraine in 1994: Third-Largest Nuclear Arsenal and Critical Military Facilities

Source: "Inheriting the Bomb" by Mariana Budjeryn

Ukraine inherited the world’s third-biggest nuclear arsenal in 1991 before giving them up as part of Budapest Memorandum, a security assurances document, signed by the US, UK and Russia.

A 2024 study found that almost three quarters of Ukrainians want them back.

Dr Mariana Budjeryn, is Senior Research Associate at the Harvard Kennedy School Belfer Centre, and author of a book about Ukraine’s nuclear disarmament, doesn’t believe the arsenal would have prevented Russia’s invasion but believes Ukraine could have protected itself better.

"It was too binary—either give up nukes or not, without considering options in between," Budjeryn argues.

"I'm pretty sure the world would have paid closer attention to Ukraine's borders for fear of its nuclear program," she concludes.

The Budapest Memorandum: A Betrayed Promise

When Ukraine signed the 1994 Budapest Memorandum, it surrendered approximately 1,900 strategic nuclear warheads for security assurances from Russia, the US, and UK. These promises crumbled when Russia annexed Crimea in 2014 and later launched a full-scale invasion, leaving Ukraine defenseless against an aggressor that had pledged to respect its borders. Recent studies show nearly three-quarters of Ukrainians now regret giving up their nuclear deterrent. In March 2022, President Zelensky bitterly noted that allies had provided only "50 tons of diesel fuel... probably so that we can burn the Budapest Memorandum."

While many in Ukraine regret their loss, 8,000 kilometres to the East, the survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombing voice against the usage of nuclear weapons.

Number of recognised survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings, since 1957

Source: Ministry of Health, Labour, and Welfare

“We have to keep telling and recording our stories to let people know what happened” insists Satoshi Tanaka, a member of the Nobel Peace Prize winning survivor group Nihon Hidankyo.

The 81-year-old survivor of Hiroshima is concerned that amid the rise of nuclear risk, people who can tell stories of what happens when the weapons are used are dwindling.

There used to be at least over 370,000 in 1980. Now, there are only just over 100,000. “It is said that in ten years, there will be no survivors left”, says Satoshi.

Photo provided by Satoshi Tanaka

Loss, Illness, Discrimination: What the Survivors Had to Face

Satoshi Tanaka entered the destroyed city of Hiroshima the day after the bombing at 1 year-old. His family and relatives suffered injury and illness due to radiation, and he had lost eleven of them, and one missing. When he was a university student, he faced a discrimination against him when a flatmate asked, “Are you sure the atomic bomb disease is not infectious?”. And since in his fifties, he suffered from six cancers. He, along with many survivors today, are afraid of cancers and illnesses that might strike. And with the rising nuclear threat, Satoshi says “they are not old stories. It is continuing today”.

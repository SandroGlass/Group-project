Overview of All Facilities

After the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, Ukraine inherited the third-largest nuclear arsenal in the world. This map shows all nuclear and military facilities in Ukraine as of 1994, before the country agreed to denuclearize under the Budapest Memorandum.

Nuclear Power Plants

Ukraine operated several nuclear power plants, including the infamous Chornobyl plant which experienced a catastrophic accident in 1986. These plants provided a significant portion of Ukraine's electricity.

Strategic Missile Divisions

Ukraine possessed intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) with nuclear warheads, primarily SS-19 and SS-24 missiles, stationed at several missile bases throughout the country.

Nuclear Warhead Storage

Several facilities across Ukraine were used to store nuclear warheads. These sites were among the primary concerns during denuclearization negotiations.

Nuclear Research Institutes

Ukraine maintained research facilities that conducted scientific work related to nuclear energy and technology, continuing the Soviet tradition of nuclear research.

Military-Industrial Enterprises

Ukraine inherited major military-industrial complexes that supported the Soviet Union's nuclear and conventional weapons programs, providing technological expertise and manufacturing capabilities.

Strategic Aviation

Ukraine possessed strategic bomber aircraft capable of delivering nuclear weapons, providing an air-based component to complement land-based missile systems.

Denuclearization

In December 1994, Ukraine signed the Budapest Memorandum, agreeing to give up all nuclear weapons inherited from the Soviet Union in exchange for security assurances from the United States, United Kingdom, and Russia. By 1996, all nuclear warheads had been transferred to Russia for dismantlement, and Ukraine officially became a non-nuclear state.

The Legacy of Denuclearization

Ukraine's decision to denuclearize made it the first country in history to voluntarily give up a nuclear arsenal. The security assurances provided in the Budapest Memorandum later became a matter of significant controversy following Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014 and the full-scale invasion in 2022.
